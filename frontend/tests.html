Introduction to Kinematics

This test covers fundamental concepts of motion in one dimension, including:

  • Displacement, Velocity, and Acceleration
  • Equations of Motion (SUVAT): \( v = u + at \), \( s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \), \( v^2 = u^2 + 2as \)
  • Motion Graphs (displacement-time, velocity-time)
  • Free Fall and Projectile Motion

Kinematics is a branch of classical mechanics that describes the motion of points, bodies (objects), and systems of bodies without considering the forces that cause them to move. Key concepts include:

  • Displacement (s): The change in position of an object. It's a vector quantity (has magnitude and direction).
  • Velocity (v): The rate of change of displacement. Also a vector quantity. Average velocity = Δs/Δt.
  • Acceleration (a): The rate of change of velocity. A vector quantity. Average acceleration = Δv/Δt.
  • Uniform Acceleration: When the acceleration is constant, we can use the SUVAT equations.
  • Free Fall: Motion under the influence of gravity alone (assuming negligible air resistance). The acceleration due to gravity (g) is approximately 9.81 m/s².

Example Problem:
A car accelerates uniformly from rest to 20 m/s in 5 seconds. Find its acceleration and the distance traveled.

Solution:

  • u = 0 m/s (initial velocity)
  • v = 20 m/s (final velocity)
  • t = 5 s (time)
  • a = ? (acceleration)
  • s = ? (distance)

Using \( v = u + at \): 20 = 0 + a × 5 ⇒ a = 4 m/s²

Using \( s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \): s = 0 × 5 + ½ × 4 × 5² ⇒ s = 50 m

Forces and Newton's Laws

This section will cover Newton's Three Laws of Motion, types of forces (friction, tension, normal force, gravity), free-body diagrams, and solving problems involving forces and motion.

  • Newton's First Law (Inertia): An object remains at rest or in uniform motion unless acted upon by an external force.
  • Newton's Second Law: \( F = ma \), where \( F \) is force, \( m \) is mass, and \( a \) is acceleration.
  • Newton's Third Law: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

Types of Forces:

  • Friction: Opposes motion between surfaces.
  • Tension: Force in a string or rope.
  • Normal Force: Perpendicular force exerted by a surface.
  • Gravity: Force of attraction between masses (\( F = mg \)).

Example Problem:
A 10 kg box is pushed with a force of 50 N. If the coefficient of friction is 0.3, find the acceleration.

Solution:

  • Normal force \( N = mg = 10 \times 9.81 = 98.1 \, N \)
  • Frictional force \( F_f = \mu N = 0.3 \times 98.1 = 29.43 \, N \)
  • Net force \( F_{net} = 50 - 29.43 = 20.57 \, N \)
  • Acceleration \( a = F_{net}/m = 20.57/10 = 2.057 \, m/s^2 \)

Energy and Work

This section will cover different forms of energy (kinetic, potential, thermal), the work-energy theorem, conservation of energy, and power.

  • Kinetic Energy (KE): \( KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \)
  • Potential Energy (PE): \( PE = mgh \) (gravitational potential energy)
  • Work-Energy Theorem: Work done = Change in kinetic energy (\( W = \Delta KE \))
  • Conservation of Energy: Total energy in a closed system remains constant.
  • Power: Rate of doing work (\( P = W/t \)).

Example Problem:
A 2 kg object is lifted to a height of 5 m. Calculate its potential energy.

Solution:
\( PE = mgh = 2 \times 9.81 \times 5 = 98.1 \, J \)

Algebra Fundamentals

This test covers the basics of algebra, including:

  • Linear Equations: Equations of the form \( ax + b = c \).
  • Quadratic Equations: \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \). Solved using:
    • Factoring
    • Quadratic formula: \( x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \)
  • Systems of Equations: Solving for multiple variables using substitution, elimination, or matrices.
  • Polynomials: Addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division.
  • Inequalities: Solving and graphing inequalities, including compound inequalities.

Example 1: Solve for x: 3x + 5 = 14

Solution: 3x = 9 ⇒ x = 3

Example 2: Solve for x: x² - 5x + 6 = 0

Solution: (x - 2)(x - 3) = 0 ⇒ x = 2 or x = 3

Example 3: Solve the system of equations:

  • 2x + y = 7
  • x - y = 2

Solution: Adding the equations, we get 3x = 9 ⇒ x = 3. Substituting into the first equation: 2(3) + y = 7 ⇒ y = 1. So the solution is x = 3, y = 1.

Calculus 1

This section will cover limits, derivatives, and basic integration techniques. Key concepts include the power rule, chain rule, product rule, and quotient rule for differentiation, and the power rule and basic u-substitution for integration.

Derivatives:

  • Power rule: \( \frac{d}{dx}x^n = nx^{n-1} \)
  • Chain rule: \( \frac{d}{dx}f(g(x)) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \)

Integrals:

  • Power rule: \( \int x^n \, dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C \)
  • Substitution: \( \int f(g(x))g'(x) \, dx = \int f(u) \, du \)

Example Problem:
Find the derivative of \( f(x) = 3x^2 + 2x + 1 \).

Solution:
\( f'(x) = 6x + 2 \)

Geometry Basics

This section will cover fundamental geometric shapes (triangles, circles, quadrilaterals, etc.), area and perimeter calculations, the Pythagorean theorem, and basic trigonometry (sine, cosine, tangent).

  • Triangles: Area = \( \frac{1}{2} \times base \times height \)
  • Circles: Area = \( \pi r^2 \), Circumference = \( 2\pi r \)
  • Pythagorean Theorem: \( a^2 + b^2 = c^2 \) (for right triangles)
  • Trigonometry: \( \sin \theta = \frac{opposite}{hypotenuse} \), \( \cos \theta = \frac{adjacent}{hypotenuse} \), \( \tan \theta = \frac{opposite}{adjacent} \)

Example Problem:
Find the area of a circle with radius 7 cm.

Solution:
\( A = \pi r^2 = \pi \times 7^2 = 49\pi \, cm^2 \)

Atomic Structure and Bonding

This test covers the fundamentals of atomic structure and chemical bonding, including:

  • Structure of the Atom: Protons, neutrons, and electrons. Atomic number and mass number. Isotopes.
  • Electron Configuration: Arrangement of electrons in orbitals (e.g., \( 1s^2 2s^2 2p^6 \)).
  • Ionic Bonding: Transfer of electrons between atoms to form ions (e.g., NaCl).
  • Covalent Bonding: Sharing of electrons between atoms (e.g., H₂O).
  • Lewis Structures: Diagrams showing valence electrons.
  • VSEPR Theory: Predicts molecular shapes based on electron repulsion.

Example: Draw the Lewis structure for water (H₂O).

Solution: Oxygen has 6 valence electrons, and each hydrogen has 1. Oxygen forms two single bonds with the hydrogen atoms and has two lone pairs of electrons. The Lewis structure shows this arrangement.

Example: What is the electron configuration of oxygen?

Solution: Oxygen has 8 electrons. The electron configuration is 1s²2s²2p&sup4;.

Example: What type of bond is formed between sodium (Na) and chlorine (Cl)?

Solution: Sodium readily loses an electron to form Na⁺, and chlorine readily gains an electron to form Cl⁻. The electrostatic attraction between these oppositely charged ions forms an ionic bond.

Stoichiometry

This section will cover mole calculations, balancing chemical equations, limiting reactants, and percent yield.

  • Mole Calculations: \( n = \frac{m}{M} \) (moles = mass/molar mass)
  • Balancing Equations: Ensure the same number of atoms of each element on both sides of the equation.
  • Limiting Reactants: The reactant that is completely consumed in a chemical reaction, thus determining the maximum amount of product that can be formed.
  • Percent Yield: \( \frac{actual \, yield}{theoretical \, yield} \times 100 \)

Example Problem:
Balance the following chemical equation: \( H_2 + O_2 \rightarrow H_2O \).

Solution:
The balanced equation is: \( 2H_2 + O_2 \rightarrow 2H_2O \)

Acids and Bases

This section will cover the properties of acids and bases, pH calculations, titrations, and buffer solutions.

  • Acids: Donate protons (\( H^+ \)) in solution.
  • Bases: Accept protons (\( H^+ \)) in solution.
  • pH Scale: A measure of the acidity or basicity of a solution. \( pH = -\log[H^+] \)
  • Titrations: A technique used to determine the concentration of an unknown acid or base by reacting it with a solution of known concentration.
  • Buffer Solutions: Solutions that resist changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added.

Example Problem:
Calculate the pH of a solution with a hydrogen ion concentration of \( [H^+] = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, M \).

Solution:
\( pH = -\log(1 \times 10^{-3}) = 3 \)
